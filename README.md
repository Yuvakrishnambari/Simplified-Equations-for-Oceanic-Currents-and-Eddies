## Thesis Overview

Oceanic currents and mesoscale eddies play a critical role in global climate, heat transport, and marine ecosystems.
However, the full governing equations of fluid motion are often too complex to analyze directly.

Objective

The primary goal of this thesis is to:

Derive simplified dynamical equations for oceanic currents and eddies

Analyze their stability and behavior

Study how scaling, non-dimensionalisation, and physical assumptions influence flow dynamics

This work bridges applied mathematics, physics, and data-driven reasoning, and demonstrates strong analytical and problem-solving skills.

## Methodology

The research follows a structured analytical workflow:

**Governing Equations**

Started from fundamental fluid dynamics equations used in geophysical flows

Identified dominant physical forces relevant to large-scale ocean motion

**Non-Dimensionalisation & Scaling**

Applied scaling analysis to reduce equation complexity

Introduced dimensionless parameters to isolate key dynamics

**Model Simplification**

Derived reduced-order equations under physically meaningful assumptions

Focused on tractable models for currents and eddies

**Stability & Dynamical Analysis**

Studied equilibrium solutions

Analyzed stability properties of the simplified systems

Interpreted physical meaning of mathematical results

**Reference Framework**

The theoretical foundation of this thesis is based on established literature in geophysical fluid dynamics, particularly:

Classical fluid dynamics theory

Rotating fluid systems

Ocean circulation models

A primary reference text used throughout the work is included in the repository for academic context.
